Antiplatelets — Primary Hemostasis Targets

Aspirin (ASA) COX Inhibitor PO · 81mg (antiplatelet) / 325mg (anti-inflam)
⚙ MOA
Irreversibly inhibits COX-1 (and COX-2) → ↓ thromboxane A₂ (TXA₂) synthesis in platelets → ↓ vasoconstriction and platelet aggregation.

Rapid onset: platelet inhibition within 1 hour. Effect lasts platelet lifetime (7–10 days) because platelets cannot synthesize new COX.
⚡ Side Effects & Contraindications
GI ulcers/bleeding — COX-1 also makes prostaglandins that protect gastric mucosa

Renal impairment — prostaglandins support renal perfusion

Contraindicated: children with viral illness (Reye syndrome); hemorrhagic stroke
🎯 Clinical Uses
ACS Ischemic Stroke PAD 1° / 2° CV prevention

During acute MI/stroke: ↓ future vascular events by ~20%
Safe in pregnancy for specific high-clotting risks (e.g., APS)
⭐ High-Yield Facts
Irreversible COX-1 — effect lasts 7–10 days (platelet lifetime). Surgery: hold 7–10 days pre-op.
Low dose (81 mg) = antiplatelet only. High dose = anti-inflammatory.
ACS → DAPT: aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor (dual antiplatelet therapy).
Ischemic stroke without tPA → aspirin alone. Post-tPA → hold aspirin 24 hrs, then resume.
Aspirin is NOT for hemorrhagic stroke — always confirm ischemic. Never in children with viral illness → Reye syndrome.
Clopidogrel / Prasugrel P2Y12 Inhibitor — Thienopyridine (Prodrug) PO
⚙ MOA
Prodrug → hepatic CYP2C19 activation → active metabolite irreversibly blocks P2Y12 (ADP receptor) on platelets → ↓ ADP-induced platelet aggregation.

Prasugrel (3rd gen): also a prodrug but more consistently activated; faster onset; stronger inhibition.
⚡ Side Effects & Contraindications
Bleeding — DAPT (clopidogrel + aspirin) higher bleeding risk than ASA alone

Clopidogrel contraindication/caution: CYP2C19 poor metabolizers (↓ efficacy)

Surgery: hold clopidogrel 5–7 days pre-op
🎯 Clinical Uses
ACS Post-PCI stent PAD Ischemic stroke (2° prevention)

Used for ~1 year post-MI (with ASA); loading dose during active ACS
⭐ High-Yield Facts
Prodrug: requires CYP2C19 to become active. Poor metabolizers (~15% Caucasian, ~30% Asian) → ↓ platelet inhibition → ↑ thrombosis risk.
FDA black box warning: test for CYP2C19 loss-of-function in high-risk patients.
Clopidogrel = irreversible P2Y12 block (lasts platelet lifetime).
Prasugrel: stronger/faster; preferred if high ischemic risk and no prior stroke/TIA (higher bleeding).
Do NOT use clopidogrel in CYP2C19 poor metabolizers — switch to ticagrelor (direct-acting, bypasses this issue).
Ticagrelor P2Y12 Inhibitor — Direct-Acting (NOT a prodrug) PO
⚙ MOA
Direct-acting (no hepatic activation needed) → reversibly blocks P2Y12 (ADP receptor) → ↓ platelet aggregation.

Faster onset than clopidogrel. More complete inhibition of ADP response. More efficacious than clopidogrel with similar bleeding risk (PLATO trial → ↓ mortality vs. clopidogrel in ACS).
⚡ Side Effects & Contraindications
Dyspnea — unique to ticagrelor; non-bronchospastic mechanism (adenosine-mediated); does not require stopping

Bleeding — similar to clopidogrel

More expensive than clopidogrel
🎯 Clinical Uses
ACS (preferred P2Y12) Post-PCI stent

Preferred over clopidogrel in ACS (PLATO trial: ↓ MI, stroke, CV death)
⭐ High-Yield Facts
Not a prodrug → works regardless of CYP2C19 status. Preferred in ACS over clopidogrel.
Reversible P2Y12 block (vs. irreversible for clopidogrel/prasugrel).
Unique ADR: dyspnea (adenosine-mediated, NOT bronchospasm — don't confuse with asthma).
PLATO trial: ticagrelor superior to clopidogrel in ACS (lower mortality).
Ticagrelor dyspnea ≠ bronchospasm. Patient complaining of shortness of breath on ticagrelor → do NOT automatically switch; assess first.
Eptifibatide (GP IIb/IIIa Inhibitor) GP IIb/IIIa Antagonist IV only
⚙ MOA
Blocks GP IIb/IIIa receptor (final common pathway of platelet aggregation) → prevents fibrinogen and vWF from bridging platelets → ↓ platelet aggregation.

GP IIb/IIIa changes shape when activated, allowing platelet-fibrin binding — eptifibatide prevents this conformational change.
⚡ Side Effects & Contraindications
Bleeding — most significant risk

Thrombocytopenia (rare but monitor platelets)

IV only — cannot be given orally
🎯 Clinical Uses
ACS (adjunct) PCI procedures

Used periprocedurally; rapid onset and offset (short t½)
⭐ High-Yield Facts
IV only — all other antiplatelets are oral. Rapid onset AND rapid offset.
Blocks the final common pathway of platelet aggregation — most "downstream" antiplatelet.
Used primarily in PCI/ACS settings — not for outpatient long-term use.
GP IIb/IIIa inhibitors carry higher bleeding risk than P2Y12 inhibitors and are reserved for in-hospital procedural settings.

Heparin-Derived Anticoagulants

Heparin (UFH — Unfractionated) Indirect Thrombin Inhibitor IV or SQ · Monitored by aPTT
⚙ MOA
Binds antithrombin III (AT-III) via unique pentasaccharide sequence → activates AT-III 1000× → AT-III inactivates thrombin (IIa), Factor Xa, and Factor IXa.

Thrombin inhibition requires longer heparin chains (to bridge AT-III to thrombin); Xa inhibition only needs pentasaccharide binding → variable chain length = unpredictable dosing.
⚡ Side Effects & Contraindications
Bleeding — main risk; reverse with protamine sulfate (full reversal)

HIT Type II — IgG ab vs. heparin-PF4 complex → platelet activation → paradoxical thrombosis + thrombocytopenia (days 5–14)

Osteoporosis (long-term use)
🎯 Clinical Uses
DVT/PE (acute) ACS Renal failure Surgery bridging Dialysis

Preferred over LMWH in: ESRD/CKD (not renally cleared), patients needing rapid reversal, hemodynamically unstable
⭐ High-Yield Facts
Monitor with aPTT (goal 1.5–2.5× normal). NOT anti-Xa.
Safe in renal failure (cleared by reticuloendothelial system, not kidneys).
Does NOT cross placenta → safe in pregnancy (but LMWH preferred).
Reversed by protamine sulfate (full reversal). Can stop 4–6 hrs before procedure.
HIT: platelet drop ≥50%, days 5–14, paradoxical THROMBOSIS → STOP heparin → argatroban/bivalirudin.
In HIT: do NOT switch to LMWH (cross-reacts). Do NOT give warfarin acutely (protein C/S depletion → skin necrosis). Do NOT transfuse platelets.
Enoxaparin (LMWH) Low-Molecular-Weight Heparin SQ only · Anti-Xa monitoring if needed
⚙ MOA
Same mechanism as UFH (binds AT-III) but smaller chains → primarily inhibits Factor Xa only (insufficient chain length to bridge AT-III to thrombin).

More predictable pharmacokinetics → weight-based fixed dosing, no routine monitoring. Renally cleared.
⚡ Side Effects & Contraindications
Bleeding
HIT — lower risk than UFH (shorter chains less likely to bind PF4), but can still occur

Contraindicated: CrCl <15–30 mL/min / ESRD (accumulates → bleeding risk)

Partial reversal only with protamine (~60% anti-Xa reversed)
🎯 Clinical Uses
DVT/PE (Tx & ppx) ACS Pregnancy Outpatient bridging

Preferred for acute PE over UFH (unless hemodynamically unstable). Preferred in pregnancy (doesn't cross placenta; safer PK).
⭐ High-Yield Facts
Monitor with anti-Xa levels (NOT aPTT). Only monitor in pregnancy, obesity, renal impairment.
Renally cleared → AVOID in GFR <30 (accumulates). Use UFH instead.
Does NOT cross placenta → preferred anticoagulant in pregnancy.
Lower HIT risk than UFH but still possible — stop if HIT suspected.
Do NOT use LMWH in severe CKD (GFR <30) — use UFH. Partial protamine reversal only (~60%). LMWH is cross-reactive in HIT — never substitute for UFH in HIT.

Vitamin K Antagonist (VKA)

Warfarin Vitamin K Epoxide Reductase Inhibitor PO · INR monitoring
⚙ MOA
Inhibits Vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKOR) → ↓ recycling of vitamin K → ↓ γ-carboxylation of factors II, VII, IX, X (and Protein C, S).

Factor VII shortest t½ → INR rises first. Full anticoagulant effect takes 2–5 days. Metabolized by CYP2C9 (many drug interactions).
⚡ Side Effects & Contraindications
Bleeding — narrow therapeutic index; monitor INR
Teratogen (Category X) — fetal warfarin syndrome (6–10% congenital abnormalities); use LMWH in pregnancy
Skin/tissue necrosis — protein C/S depleted first → hypercoagulability (especially in HIT transition)

Many drug interactions: CYP2C9 inhibitors ↑ INR (amiodarone, azoles, fluoroquinolones); CYP2C9 inducers ↓ INR (rifampin, anti-epileptics); High vitamin K diet ↓ efficacy
🎯 Clinical Uses
Mechanical heart valves ★ONLY★ DVT/PE Afib APS

INR goal: 2–3 (most indications); 2.5–3.5 (mechanical valves).
Still used when DOACs not affordable.
⭐ High-Yield Facts
Inhibits factors II, VII, IX, X + Protein C, S. Factor VII shortest t½ → INR rises first.
Mechanical heart valves → WARFARIN ONLY. DOACs contraindicated (RE-ALIGN trial).
Reversal: mild → hold + Vit K PO; serious → FFP + Vit K IV; life-threatening → 4-factor PCC + Vit K IV (fastest).
Amiodarone inhibits CYP2C9 → ↑ warfarin → ↑ INR (classic interaction; effect persists months due to amiodarone's long t½).
Teratogenic — use LMWH in pregnancy. Skin necrosis in HIT transition — protein C shortest t½.
FFP alone is NOT sufficient reversal — provides factors immediately but transiently. Must add Vitamin K for sustained effect. DOACs are NOT approved for mechanical valves.

Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs / NOACs)

Rivaroxaban · Apixaban · Edoxaban Direct Factor Xa Inhibitors PO · No routine monitoring
⚙ MOA
Directly inhibit Factor Xa → ↓ thrombin generation → ↓ fibrin clot formation. No vitamin K dependence, no AT-III required.

Predictable PK → no routine monitoring. All oral. Renal clearance varies: Apixaban least (27%), Rivaroxaban moderate, Edoxaban moderate.
⚡ Side Effects & Contraindications
Bleeding (limited reversal vs. warfarin)

Renal dosing adjustments required
Apixaban: safest in CKD (only 27% renal clearance)
Rivaroxaban/Edoxaban: moderate renal clearance — reduce/avoid in severe CKD

Contraindicated: mechanical heart valves
🎯 Clinical Uses
Non-valvular Afib (preferred) DVT/PE Tx & ppx

Preferred over warfarin for non-valvular Afib (ROCKET-AF, ARISTOTLE, ENGAGE trials).
Apixaban also for long-term recurrent DVT/PE prevention.
⭐ High-Yield Facts
Reversal: andexanet alfa (recombinant Xa decoy — specific antidote for Xa inhibitors).
Apixaban preferred in CKD — only 27% renally cleared (vs. dabigatran ~80%).
Preferred over warfarin for non-valvular Afib — no monitoring, fewer drug interactions, equal/better efficacy.
No routine monitoring — occasional anti-Xa level if needed (e.g., urgent surgery, renal impairment).
Do NOT use any DOAC for mechanical heart valves — warfarin ONLY. All DOACs are renally cleared to varying degrees; adjust in CKD.
Dabigatran (Pradaxa) Direct Thrombin (IIa) Inhibitor — DOAC PO · No routine monitoring
⚙ MOA
Directly inhibits thrombin (Factor IIa) → ↓ fibrin formation. Most renally cleared of all DOACs (~80% renal).

No prodrug activation required. Predictable PK. No vitamin K dependence.
⚡ Side Effects & Contraindications
Bleeding
GI upset — take with food
Contraindicated in severe renal failure (~80% renal clearance → accumulates)
Contraindicated: mechanical heart valves (RE-ALIGN trial showed inferiority)
🎯 Clinical Uses
Non-valvular Afib DVT/PE Tx & ppx

Preferred over warfarin for non-valvular Afib (RE-LY trial — non-inferior/superior to warfarin)
⭐ High-Yield Facts
Reversal: idarucizumab (Praxbind) — specific monoclonal antibody antidote. Name to memorize.
Most renally cleared DOAC (~80%) → most affected by CKD. Avoid in severe renal failure.
GI upset → take with food. GI absorption more variable than Xa inhibitors.
RE-LY trial: dabigatran non-inferior/superior to warfarin for non-valvular Afib.
RE-ALIGN trial: dabigatran shown INFERIOR to warfarin in mechanical valves with more thromboembolic AND bleeding events. Mechanical valves = warfarin ONLY, always.

Direct Thrombin Inhibitors (IV) — HIT Management

Argatroban · Bivalirudin Direct Thrombin Inhibitors — IV IV · Very short t½
⚙ MOA
Directly inhibit thrombin (Factor IIa) → ↓ fibrin formation. NOT heparin-derived → do not cross-react with HIT antibodies.

Bivalirudin: reversible direct thrombin inhibitor; very short t½. Argatroban: hepatically cleared (preferred in renal failure).
⚡ Side Effects & Contraindications
Bleeding

Argatroban: hepatically metabolized → use with caution in liver failure, but preferred in renal failure
Bivalirudin: renally cleared → reduce dose in CKD
🎯 Clinical Uses
HIT (first-line replacement) PCI (bivalirudin)

Used to deactivate the thrombin already activated by HIT. Bridge to warfarin/DOAC once platelets recover.
⭐ High-Yield Facts
These are the go-to drugs for HIT — not heparin-derived, so no cross-reactivity.
Argatroban preferred in renal failure (hepatically cleared). Bivalirudin preferred in liver failure (renally cleared).
Transition to warfarin ONLY after platelets recover in HIT — never start warfarin acutely.
In HIT: NEVER use LMWH (cross-reacts), NEVER transfuse platelets (worsens thrombosis), NEVER start warfarin acutely (depletes protein C → skin necrosis/worsening thrombosis).

Fibrinolytics (Thrombolytics)

Alteplase (tPA) · Tenecteplase Tissue Plasminogen Activator — Fibrinolytic IV infusion · Fast-acting
⚙ MOA
Converts plasminogen → plasmin → plasmin cleaves fibrin crosslinks → dissolves clot.

Must be given early — as fibrin matures and crosslinks form, plasmin is less effective. Tenecteplase: longer t½, bolus dosing (vs. infusion for alteplase). D-dimer ↑ after tPA = expected (fibrin degradation products).
⚡ Side Effects & Contraindications
Intracranial hemorrhage — most feared complication
Major bleeding

Absolute contraindications:
• Prior intracranial hemorrhage (any time)
• Active internal bleeding
• Recent (<3 mo) intracranial surgery or head trauma
• Ischemic stroke within 3 months
• BP >185/110 (for stroke indication)
• Suspected aortic dissection
🎯 Clinical Uses
Ischemic stroke (<4.5 hrs) STEMI (if PCI >120 min) Massive PE (unstable)

Always do CT head first for stroke to rule out hemorrhage before giving tPA.
⭐ High-Yield Facts
Ischemic stroke: tPA within 4.5 hours of symptom onset (or last known well time). CT head must be done FIRST.
BP threshold for stroke tPA: <185/110 (not 140/90 — common confusion).
STEMI: tPA if primary PCI not available within 120 minutes of first medical contact.
After tPA in stroke: hold antiplatelets for 24 hours, then start aspirin.
Last known well time (not symptom recognition) determines eligibility window.
tPA in hemorrhagic stroke = catastrophic and potentially fatal. CT MUST be done first, always. Daily aspirin is NOT a contraindication to tPA. Prior hemorrhagic stroke = absolute contraindication regardless of how long ago.

🔄 Reversal Agents — Quick Reference

UFH (Heparin) → Protamine Sulfate Full reversal
LMWH (Enoxaparin) → Protamine Sulfate Partial reversal (~60% anti-Xa)
Warfarin (mild/no bleed) → Hold + Vitamin K (PO) Slow onset (hours)
Warfarin (serious bleed) → FFP + Vitamin K IV FFP = immediate, transient
Warfarin (life-threatening) → 4-factor PCC + Vit K IV Fastest, most complete
Dabigatran → Idarucizumab (Praxbind) Specific monoclonal Ab antidote
Apixaban / Rivaroxaban → Andexanet Alfa Recombinant Xa decoy
