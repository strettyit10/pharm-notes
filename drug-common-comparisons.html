Anticoagulation / antiplatelet
Aspirin vs clopidogrel
High frequency ▶
Aspirin
Blocks COX-1 → inhibits TXA2 synthesis
Irreversibly acetylates COX-1
Effect lasts 7-10 days (platelet lifetime)
First drug to give in ACS
Does NOT affect Protein C/S
Clopidogrel
Blocks P2Y12 ADP receptor on platelets
Irreversibly binds receptor
Effect lasts 7-10 days (platelet lifetime)
Prodrug — requires CYP2C19 activation
Used with aspirin post-stent (DAPT)
Key distinction: Aspirin → TXA2 pathway. Clopidogrel → ADP pathway. Different targets, synergistic when combined. Both irreversible. Aspirin goes first in ACS.
Heparin (UFH) vs argatroban
High frequency ▶
Heparin (UFH)
Indirect — activates antithrombin III
Requires aPTT monitoring
Causes HIT (immune, IgG vs heparin-PF4)
Reversed by protamine sulfate
Affected by platelet factor 4
Argatroban
Direct thrombin inhibitor (no ATIII needed)
No interaction with platelet factor 4
First-line when HIT is present
Hepatic elimination
No reversal agent
Key distinction: HIT = platelet drop >50% after day 5-10 of heparin. Stop all heparin (including LMWH). Start argatroban or bivalirudin. HIT is immune-mediated (unpredictable, NOT dose-dependent) and life-threatening (paradoxical thrombosis).
Warfarin vs DOACs (apixaban, dabigatran)
High frequency ▶
Warfarin
Inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase
Blocks factors II, VII, IX, X + Protein C/S
Early hypercoagulable state (protein C drops first)
Requires INR monitoring (goal 2-3)
Many drug/food interactions
Can use for mechanical heart valves
Reversed by FFP (immediate) or vitamin K
DOACs (apixaban/rivaroxaban/dabigatran)
Target Factor Xa (apixaban, rivaroxaban) or IIa (dabigatran)
No routine monitoring required
Fewer drug interactions
NOT approved for mechanical heart valves
Reversal: andexanet alfa (Xa), idarucizumab (dabigatran)
Key distinction: DOACs more specific, no monitoring, can't use for mechanical valves. INR 1.4 = under-anticoagulated (thrombosis risk). INR 5 = over-anticoagulated (bleeding risk, decreased clotting).
tPA (alteplase) vs anticoagulants
High frequency ▶
tPA (alteplase)
Converts plasminogen → plasmin
Dissolves EXISTING formed clot
STEMI with no cath lab within 120 min
Ischemic stroke within 3-4.5 hours
Major bleeding risk (including ICH)
Anticoagulants (heparin, DOACs, warfarin)
Prevent NEW clot formation
Do NOT dissolve existing clots
Long-term AF stroke prevention
DVT/PE treatment and prevention
Bridge therapy peri-procedure
Key distinction: tPA = thrombolytic (dissolves clot). Anticoagulants = prevent new clot. tPA is NOT used for AF stroke prevention — that requires anticoagulants. For AF + prior stroke → apixaban immediately.
Angina drugs
Nitrates vs beta-blockers: mechanism & contraindications
High frequency ▶
Nitrates (nitroglycerin, isosorbide)
NO → guanylate cyclase → cGMP → vasodilation
Primarily venodilation → reduces preload
Redistributes blood to subendocardium (↓LVEDP)
Does NOT reduce mortality post-MI
Tolerance with continuous use → nitrate-free interval
Contraindicated with sildenafil (PDE5 inhibitors)
Cause reflex tachycardia (blocked by beta-blockers)
Beta-blockers (metoprolol, atenolol)
Block β1 → reduce HR, contractility, O2 demand
Reduce reflex tachycardia from nitrates
REDUCE mortality post-MI
CONTRAINDICATED in variant angina (worsen spasm)
Atenolol: less CNS SE (less lipophilic than metoprolol)
Safe in stable angina, NOT variant angina
Critical distinction: Nitrates do NOT reduce mortality post-MI (unlike beta-blockers). Beta-blockers are contraindicated in variant (vasospastic) angina. Nitrates cause tolerance — need 8-12hr nitrate-free period (remove patch at night).
DHP CCBs (amlodipine) vs non-DHP CCBs (diltiazem, verapamil)
High frequency ▶
Amlodipine (DHP)
Blocks L-type Ca channels in VASCULAR smooth muscle
Arteriolar dilation → reduces afterload
Does NOT slow AV node → NOT for AF rate control
Causes peripheral edema (arteriolar, not fluid retention)
Safe in asthma, safe with NSAIDs
Treats variant angina (coronary vasodilation)
Diltiazem / Verapamil (non-DHP)
Block Ca channels in CARDIAC tissue AND vasculature
Slow AV node → use for AF/flutter rate control + angina
Inhibit CYP3A4 → increase atorvastatin levels (use pravastatin)
CONTRAINDICATED with beta-blockers (AV block)
Verapamil: constipation; diltiazem: preferred for angina + AF
Both treat variant angina
Key distinction: Only non-DHPs slow the AV node. Diltiazem = best drug for angina + AF. Verapamil/diltiazem + beta-blocker = dangerous AV block. Amlodipine edema is arteriolar (not fluid retention — diuretics don't fix it).
Hypertension drugs
ACE inhibitors vs ARBs
High frequency ▶
ACE inhibitors (lisinopril, enalapril)
Block ACE → reduce Ang II AND accumulate bradykinin
Bradykinin → dry cough (10-15%) and angioedema
Reduce intraglomerular pressure (dilate efferent arteriole)
Enalapril is a prodrug (delayed peak effect)
Reduce new-onset diabetes
Both AT1 and AT2 receptors receive less Ang II
ARBs (losartan, valsartan)
Block AT1 receptor → Ang II shunted to AT2
GREATEST AT2 receptor stimulation (Ang II builds up)
No cough (no bradykinin accumulation)
Same mortality benefit as ACE inhibitors
Renin↑, Ang I↑, Ang II↑, Aldosterone↓
Both equally effective at lowering BP
Key distinctions: Cough → switch ACE to ARB. ARBs cause greater AT2 stimulation. NEVER combine ACE + ARB (hyperkalemia + renal failure). Both contraindicated in pregnancy and bilateral renal artery stenosis. Both decrease new-onset diabetes (NOT thiazides/beta-blockers which increase it).
Loop diuretics (furosemide) vs thiazides (chlorthalidone)
High frequency ▶
Furosemide (loop)
Blocks Na/K/2Cl in thick ascending loop of Henle
Ca↓, Mg↓, K↓, Na↓ — washes out everything
Used in acute pulmonary edema, hypercalcemia
Hypokalemia → worsens digoxin toxicity
Metabolic alkalosis
No anti-remodeling, does NOT increase EF
Chlorthalidone / HCTZ (thiazide)
Blocks Na/Cl in distal convoluted tubule
Ca↑ (SAVES calcium), K↓, Na↓
Used for kidney stones (Ca type), osteoporosis, HTN
Metabolic effects: ↑glucose, ↑uric acid, ↑lipids
Does NOT decrease cholesterol
NSAIDs blunt its antihypertensive effect
Critical distinction: Furosemide → Ca DECREASES. Thiazides → Ca INCREASES. This is the single most-tested difference between these two classes.
Spironolactone / eplerenone vs triamterene (K-sparing diuretics)
Medium frequency ▶
Spironolactone / Eplerenone
Block aldosterone at mineralocorticoid receptor
↓ Na/K ATPase expression on basolateral side
Anti-remodeling benefit in HF (blocks MR in heart)
Spironolactone: gynecomastia; eplerenone: more selective
No metabolic acidosis
Triamterene / Amiloride
Directly block ENaC on luminal side
Reduce Na reabsorption → reduce H+ secretion
Cause metabolic ACIDOSIS (distinguish from others)
No cardiac anti-remodeling benefit
Hyperkalemia risk when combined with ARBs or ACE inhibitors
Key distinction: Triamterene causes metabolic acidosis (reduced H+ secretion). Spironolactone/eplerenone do not. The HF mortality benefit of aldosterone antagonists comes from blocking the receptor in the MYOCARDIUM (anti-fibrotic), not just the kidney.
Heart failure drugs
Digoxin vs ACE inhibitors / beta-blockers in HF
High frequency ▶
Digoxin
Inhibits Na/K ATPase → ↑intracellular Ca → positive inotropy
Increases vagal tone → slows AV node (AF rate control)
CO↑, SV↑, HR↓, PVR↑ (reflex vasoconstriction)
Reduces symptoms and hospitalizations
Does NOT reduce mortality (DIG trial)
Hypokalemia → ↑digoxin toxicity (yellow-green halos, AV block)
ACE inhibitors + beta-blockers (carvedilol)
ACE inhibitors: venodilation + natriuresis + anti-remodeling
Carvedilol: α1 + β block → ↓TPR, ↓HR, ↓renin, ↑K+
Both: REDUCE mortality in HFrEF
Both increase EF with chronic therapy (reverse remodeling)
Carvedilol: do NOT start in acute decompensated HF
Critical distinction: Digoxin = symptom benefit, NO mortality benefit. ACE inhibitors + beta-blockers = mortality benefit. Spironolactone/eplerenone also reduce mortality (block aldosterone in heart). Furosemide = symptoms only, no mortality benefit.
Antiarrhythmic drugs
Amiodarone vs flecainide vs lidocaine vs adenosine
High frequency ▶
Amiodarone (Class III)
Multi-class: I + II + III + IV effects
Blocks IKr → prolongs QT (rarely causes torsade)
Slows accessory pathways → safe in WPW
Best for resistant VT/VF and AF rhythm control
Half-life 40-55 days; active metabolite
SE: pulmonary fibrosis, thyroid (hypo + hyper), NOT lupus
Avoid if thyroid disease history
Flecainide (Class IC)
Slows Na+ channel recovery (slow conduction)
Increases pacing AND defibrillation threshold
Paradoxical ventricular acceleration in atrial flutter
Proarrhythmic in structural heart disease (CAST trial)
Only for structurally NORMAL hearts
Does NOT cause EADs (contrast with ibutilide)
Key distinctions: Lidocaine (Class IB) = IV only, acute VT, ischemia-selective. Adenosine = IV bolus, AVNRT/AVRT, 10-sec half-life, safe in COPD. Torsade de pointes = IKr blockade → IV magnesium. Lupus = procainamide (NOT amiodarone).
Early afterdepolarizations (EADs) vs delayed afterdepolarizations (DADs)
Medium frequency ▶
EADs → Torsade de pointes
Caused by IKr blockade (prolonged QT)
Associated: ibutilide, dofetilide, sotalol, quinidine
Also: hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, long QT syndrome
Treatment: IV magnesium (regardless of Mg level)
Also treat by increasing HR (isoproterenol, pacing)
DADs → Digoxin toxicity arrhythmias
Caused by intracellular Ca2+ overload
Classic cause: digoxin toxicity (+ hypokalemia)
Triggered activity, not re-entry
Treatment: correct electrolytes, digoxin Fab antibodies
Digoxin toxicity: yellow-green halos, AV block, nausea
Key distinction: Digoxin overdose causes DADs (calcium overload), NOT EADs. The exam explicitly tests this. EADs → torsade → IV magnesium. Flecainide does NOT cause EADs; ibutilide DOES.
