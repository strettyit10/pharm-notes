Nitroglycerin — Sublingual (SL) Nitrate · Acute SL tablet / spray · 0.3–0.4 mg PRN
⚙ MOA
Organic nitrate → releases NO → activates guanylate cyclase → ↑ cGMP → vascular smooth muscle relaxation

Primary: venodilation (↓ preload >> afterload) → ↓ wall stress → ↓ MVO₂
Secondary: coronary artery dilation → ↑ O₂ supply
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Headache — most common ADR (meningeal vasodilation)
Hypotension, reflex tachycardia, flushing

CI: PDE-5 inhibitors
Sildenafil ≥24 hr washout
Tadalafil ≥48 hr washout

CI: RV infarct (preload-dependent RV; NTG → ↓ preload → ↓ CO → hemodynamic collapse)
💊 Clinical Use
  • Acute angina attack — abort within 1–3 min
  • Onset: 1–3 min · Duration: ~30 min
  • Give up to 3 doses q5 min; call EMS if no relief after 1st dose in ACS
  • Stable, unstable, variant angina
  • Avoid in RV infarct — suspect if inferior STEMI (II, III, aVF) + right-sided leads
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ Fastest-acting antianginal — onset 1–3 min
⭐ Primarily venodilation → ↓ preload (not arterial)
⭐ Headache = expected pharmacologic effect (NOT allergy)

⚠ TRAP NTG + sildenafil/tadalafil → fatal hypotension via additive cGMP accumulation. Absolute CI.

⚠ TRAP Inferior STEMI (ST↑ II/III/aVF) → order right-sided ECG first → if RV infarct → NTG contraindicated
Nitroglycerin — Transdermal Patch / Ointment Nitrate · Chronic Prophylaxis Transdermal patch / 2% ointment · Daily
⚙ MOA
Same as SL NTG — NO → ↑ cGMP → venodilation

Continuous systemic absorption through skin → sustained plasma levels

Tolerance: depletion of vascular sulfhydryl (–SH) groups needed for NO bioactivation + neurohormonal counter-activation (↑ SNS/RAAS)
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Headache, hypotension, reflex tachycardia
Tolerance — major limitation of continuous use
Skin irritation at patch site (ointment: messier)

CI: PDE-5 inhibitors
CI: RV infarct
💊 Clinical Use
  • Chronic stable angina prophylaxis
  • Apply patch in morning, remove after 12–14 hrs (8–12 hr nitrate-free period at night)
  • Ointment: dose-titrated, more flexible
  • NOT for acute attacks
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ Tolerance prevention = 8–12 hr nitrate-free period every night (remove patch at bedtime)
⭐ Mechanism of tolerance: sulfhydryl group depletion + neurohormonal counter-activation

⚠ TRAP Continuous 24-hr use leads to tolerance within days — efficacy lost. Must have daily nitrate-free interval.

⚠ TRAP Patch is NOT the same as SL NTG — cannot abort acute attacks; onset is too slow
Isosorbide Dinitrate (ISDN) Nitrate · Oral PO · BID–TID · Eccentric dosing
⚙ MOA
Organic nitrate → NO → ↑ cGMP → venodilation + some arterial dilation

Prodrug — undergoes hepatic first-pass metabolism to active isosorbide-2-mononitrate and isosorbide-5-mononitrate

Variable bioavailability (~20–60%) due to first-pass effect
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Headache, hypotension, reflex tachycardia
Tolerance with continuous dosing

CI: PDE-5 inhibitors
CI: RV infarct
💊 Clinical Use
  • Chronic stable angina prophylaxis
  • HFrEF (in combination with hydralazine = H-ISDN) — especially Black patients when ACEi/ARB not tolerated (A-HeFT trial)
  • Eccentric dosing (e.g., 8 AM + 2 PM) to allow nitrate-free period
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ ISDN = prodrug (first-pass metabolism) → variable bioavailability
⭐ ISDN + hydralazine = H-ISDN combination for HFrEF when ACEi/ARB not tolerated (A-HeFT trial — Black patients)

⚠ TRAP ISDN ≠ ISMN: ISDN undergoes first-pass; ISMN does not (higher bioavailability, more predictable). Know the difference.
Isosorbide Mononitrate (ISMN) Nitrate · Oral PO · QD (ER) or Eccentric BID
⚙ MOA
Organic nitrate → NO → ↑ cGMP → venodilation

Not a prodrug — active as administered, no hepatic first-pass metabolism

Bioavailability ≈ 100% → more predictable and consistent plasma levels than ISDN
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Headache, hypotension, reflex tachycardia
Tolerance (managed by nitrate-free interval)

CI: PDE-5 inhibitors
CI: RV infarct
💊 Clinical Use
  • Chronic stable angina prophylaxis
  • Extended-release: once daily with built-in nitrate-free period
  • Immediate-release: eccentric dosing (7 AM + 2 PM)
  • More predictable than ISDN due to 100% bioavailability
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ No first-pass metabolism → ~100% bioavailability → more predictable than ISDN
⭐ Extended-release taken once daily (7 AM) allows natural nitrate-free window overnight
⭐ This is the active metabolite of ISDN — the distinction between prodrug (ISDN) vs. active drug (ISMN) is frequently tested

⚠ TRAP ISMN ≠ ISDN: ISMN has no first-pass effect, ISDN does. ISMN is active drug; ISDN is prodrug.

β-Blockers — ↓ MVO₂ via ↓ HR + ↓ contractility

Propranolol β-Blocker · Non-selective PO · QD–TID (IR or LA)
⚙ MOA
Non-selective β₁ + β₂ blockade

β₁: ↓ HR (chronotropy) + ↓ contractility (inotropy) + ↓ conduction → ↓ MVO₂ + ↑ diastolic filling time → ↑ coronary perfusion

β₂: ↓ bronchodilation (bronchoconstriction risk) + ↓ peripheral vasodilation (Raynaud's risk) + masks hypoglycemia tachycardia
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Bronchoconstriction (β₂ block — avoid in asthma/COPD)
Bradycardia, fatigue, impotence, dyslipidemia
Masks hypoglycemia tachycardia (caution in DM)
Raynaud's worsening (β₂ block → ↓ peripheral vasodilation)

CI: Asthma / COPD
CI: Variant angina (Prinzmetal)
CI: Acute decompensated HF
AVOID: non-DHP CCB combo
💊 Clinical Use
  • Chronic stable angina (1st-line)
  • Migraine prophylaxis
  • Essential tremor
  • Portal hypertension (↓ splanchnic flow)
  • Thyrotoxicosis — ↓ peripheral T4→T3 conversion + ↓ sympathomimetic symptoms
  • Performance anxiety
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ Non-selective β₁ + β₂ — widest receptor profile of all β-blockers
⭐ Multiple non-cardiac uses: migraine, tremor, portal HTN, thyrotoxicosis, anxiety
⭐ Abrupt withdrawal → upregulated β-receptors → rebound angina/tachycardia/MI. Always taper.

⚠ TRAP β₂ blockade → unopposed α in coronary spasm → ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED in variant (Prinzmetal) angina

⚠ TRAP Masks tachycardia of hypoglycemia — use with caution in insulin-dependent diabetics
Metoprolol β-Blocker · β₁-Selective PO · Tartrate (IR) or Succinate (ER)
⚙ MOA
Cardioselective β₁ blockade (loses selectivity at high doses)

β₁: ↓ HR + ↓ contractility + ↓ AV conduction → ↓ MVO₂

Relative sparing of β₂ (lungs, peripheral vessels) → safer in mild-moderate asthma than propranolol, but still use with caution
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Bradycardia, fatigue, cold extremities
Less bronchoconstriction than propranolol (β₁ selective)
Impotence, dyslipidemia

CI: Variant angina
CI: Acute decompensated HF
Caution: high-dose loses β₁ selectivity
AVOID: non-DHP CCB combo
💊 Clinical Use
  • Chronic stable angina
  • HFrEF — succinate (ER) form only, proven mortality benefit (MERIT-HF)
  • Post-MI — reduces remodeling + arrhythmia risk
  • Hypertension
  • Afib rate control
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ β₁-selective (cardioselective) — safer than propranolol in lung disease
⭐ HFrEF: succinate (ER) ONLY — metoprolol tartrate (IR) is NOT proven in HF
⭐ 3 approved β-blockers for HFrEF: carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol

⚠ TRAP Do NOT start in acute decompensated HF — wait until euvolemic. Starting in ADHF → cardiogenic shock.

⚠ TRAP Metoprolol tartrate (IR) ≠ metoprolol succinate (ER) for HFrEF — only succinate is evidence-based
Atenolol β-Blocker · β₁-Selective PO · QD · Renally cleared
⚙ MOA
Cardioselective β₁ blockade

Same cardiac mechanism as metoprolol: ↓ HR + ↓ contractility → ↓ MVO₂

Hydrophilic — does not cross blood-brain barrier readily → fewer CNS side effects (less depression, nightmares) than propranolol
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Bradycardia, fatigue
Fewer CNS side effects than propranolol (hydrophilic)

CI: Variant angina
CI: Acute decompensated HF
Renally cleared — dose adjust in CKD
AVOID: non-DHP CCB combo
💊 Clinical Use
  • Chronic stable angina (first-line)
  • Hypertension (once daily dosing)
  • Microvascular angina — effective
  • Post-MI (less evidence than metoprolol succinate for HF benefit)
  • Preferred when CNS side effects are a concern (hydrophilic)
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ Hydrophilic → fewer CNS side effects (depression, insomnia) vs. lipophilic propranolol
⭐ Renally cleared → dose reduce in CKD (contrast: propranolol = hepatically metabolized)
⭐ NOT proven for HFrEF mortality benefit — only carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol are

⚠ TRAP Atenolol is NOT on the approved HFrEF β-blocker list — a common distractor. Only metoprolol succinate (not atenolol) is approved for HF.
Nebivolol β₁-Selective + NO Release PO · QD
⚙ MOA
Dual mechanism:
1. β₁-selective blockade → ↓ HR + ↓ contractility
2. Stimulates endothelial NO (eNOS) → vasodilation → ↓ SVR

Result: ↓ BP + ↓ HR without the peripheral vasoconstriction of other β-blockers → better tolerated in patients with peripheral vascular disease
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Bradycardia
Fewest metabolic side effects of all β-blockers (minimal ↑ TG, minimal ↓ HDL)
Least sexual dysfunction of all β-blockers

CI: Variant angina
CI: Acute decompensated HF
💊 Clinical Use
  • Hypertension (especially when metabolic side effects are a concern)
  • Stable angina
  • Preferred when sexual dysfunction with other β-blockers
  • Preferred when peripheral vascular disease — NO release maintains peripheral circulation
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ UNIQUE mechanism: β₁ blockade + endothelial NO release (eNOS stimulation) → vasodilation
⭐ Fewest metabolic side effects: no significant ↑ TG or ↓ HDL (unlike propranolol)
⭐ Least sexual dysfunction of all β-blockers
⭐ The "special" β-blocker — the only one that causes vasodilation via NO

⚠ TRAP The NO release mechanism distinguishes nebivolol from all other β-blockers — this is a frequent USMLE differentiator
Carvedilol α₁ + β₁ + β₂ Blocker PO · BID (IR) or QD (ER)
⚙ MOA
Non-selective β₁ + β₂ + α₁ blockade

β₁: ↓ HR + ↓ contractility → ↓ MVO₂ + ↓ cardiac remodeling
β₂: bronchoconstriction risk (like propranolol)
α₁: ↓ SVR → vasodilation → ↓ afterload

Net: most vasodilatory β-blocker; no reflex tachycardia because β₁ already blocked
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Dizziness, hypotension (α₁ vasodilation)
Bradycardia, fatigue
Bronchoconstriction (β₂ block — non-selective)

CI: Severe asthma
CI: Acute decompensated HF
CI: Variant angina
AVOID: non-DHP CCB combo
💊 Clinical Use
  • HFrEF — proven mortality benefit (COPERNICUS trial; one of 3 approved β-blockers for HF)
  • Post-MI LV dysfunction
  • Hypertension (α₁ block adds vasodilation)
  • Stable angina
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ α₁ + β₁ + β₂ blockade — triple receptor profile (widest of HF-approved β-blockers)
⭐ One of 3 β-blockers proven to ↓ mortality in HFrEF: carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol
⭐ Most vasodilatory β-blocker (α₁ block → ↓ afterload); no reflex tachycardia due to β₁ block

⚠ TRAP Non-selective → CONTRAINDICATED in variant angina (same as propranolol — unopposed α → worsens vasospasm)

⚠ TRAP Do NOT initiate in ADHF. Only start when hemodynamically stable and euvolemic.
Labetalol α₁ + β₁ + β₂ Blocker PO or IV (HTN emergency)
⚙ MOA
Non-selective β₁ + β₂ + α₁ blockade

Like carvedilol, but IV formulation available → used in acute hypertensive emergencies

α₁ block → ↓ SVR (no reflex tachycardia because β₁ also blocked → balanced BP reduction).
Ratio: β:α blockade ≈ 3:1 (oral), 7:1 (IV)
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Orthostatic hypotension, dizziness
Bradycardia, fatigue
Bronchoconstriction (β₂ non-selective)

CI: Severe asthma
CI: Cardiogenic shock
CI: Variant angina
💊 Clinical Use
  • Hypertensive emergency in pregnancy — drug of choice (IV)
  • Hypertensive emergency + aortic dissection (IV) — ↓ HR and BP simultaneously to reduce aortic shear stress
  • Pheochromocytoma crisis (α+β blockade)
  • Chronic hypertension (oral)
  • Stable angina
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ #1 drug for HTN emergency in pregnancy (IV labetalol) — safe for fetus
⭐ Aortic dissection: labetalol IV preferred — blocks both α (↓ SVR) and β (↓ HR) simultaneously → reduces aortic wall stress (dP/dt)
⭐ No reflex tachycardia (β₁ block prevents it despite vasodilation from α₁ block)

⚠ TRAP Non-selective β₂ block → contraindicated in variant angina. Also avoid in severe asthma.

⚠ TRAP Pure vasodilators (hydralazine alone, nitroprusside) worsen aortic dissection via reflex tachycardia — always use labetalol or a β-blocker first in dissection

Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

Nifedipine DHP CCB · Vascular-selective PO · IR or ER · QD–BID
⚙ MOA
Dihydropyridine (DHP) — blocks L-type Ca²⁺ channels primarily in vascular smooth muscle

↓ intracellular Ca²⁺ → arterial vasodilation → ↓ SVR → ↓ afterload

No significant AV nodal effect (in contrast to non-DHPs)
Rapid vasodilation → ↑ reflex tachycardia (especially short-acting IR form)
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Reflex tachycardia (significant — especially IR form)
Peripheral edema, flushing, headache
Dizziness, hypotension

CI: Short-acting IR — chronic stable angina (↑ sympathetic activation → worse outcomes)
Long-acting ER form is acceptable
Cannot rate-control Afib/SVT (no AV nodal effect)
💊 Clinical Use
  • Variant (Prinzmetal) angina — first-line (relieves vasospasm)
  • Hypertension (long-acting ER form)
  • Raynaud's phenomenon (peripheral vasodilation)
  • Chronic stable angina (long-acting form only)
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ Variant angina: drug of choice (along with other DHPs and non-DHPs) — relieves coronary vasospasm
⭐ DHP = vascular-selective → NO AV nodal effect → cannot rate-control Afib or SVT
⭐ Short-acting IR form associated with ↑ CV events in chronic stable angina — use ER only

⚠ TRAP Short-acting nifedipine is contraindicated for chronic stable angina — rapid vasodilation → reflex tachycardia → ↑ ischemia

⚠ TRAP DHP CCBs CANNOT rate-control Afib (no AV nodal effect) — must use non-DHP CCB or β-blocker for rate control
Amlodipine DHP CCB · Long-acting PO · QD · Longest t½ of all CCBs
⚙ MOA
DHP — blocks vascular smooth muscle L-type Ca²⁺ channels → arterial vasodilation → ↓ SVR

Slowest onset of all CCBs → minimal reflex tachycardia (gradual vasodilation)

t½ ≈ 35–50 hrs → once-daily dosing, smooth BP/HR effect

No AV nodal effect
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Peripheral edema — most common ADR (vasodilation → fluid redistribution, NOT cardiac edema)
Mild reflex tachycardia (less than nifedipine due to slow onset)
Flushing, headache

✓ Safe in HFrEF — only DHP CCB that does NOT worsen systolic function
Cannot rate-control Afib/SVT
💊 Clinical Use
  • Chronic stable angina
  • Variant (Prinzmetal) angina
  • Hypertension (first-line, especially elderly + isolated systolic)
  • Raynaud's phenomenon
  • HFrEF — only DHP CCB approved (PRAISE trial)
  • Angina + HFrEF (non-DHPs contraindicated in HFrEF)
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ Longest t½ of all CCBs (~40 hrs) → once daily, smooth effect, minimal reflex tachycardia
⭐ Only DHP CCB safe in HFrEF — does not worsen systolic function (non-DHPs like verapamil/diltiazem are contraindicated in HFrEF)
⭐ Peripheral edema is from vasodilation (venous pooling), NOT worsening heart failure

⚠ TRAP Amlodipine edema ≠ HF — do not stop amlodipine or add diuretic for the edema. Reducing dose or switching is the fix.

⚠ TRAP Simvastatin + amlodipine → ↑ simvastatin levels (CYP3A4 competition) → myopathy risk → max simvastatin dose 20 mg with amlodipine
Verapamil Non-DHP CCB · Most cardiac effect PO or IV · TID–QD (SR)
⚙ MOA
Non-DHP — blocks L-type Ca²⁺ channels in both vascular smooth muscle AND cardiac tissue

Heart: ↓ SA node automaticity + ↓ AV node conduction velocity + ↓ contractility (negative chronotropy + dromotropy + inotropy)

Vasculature: ↓ SVR (less vasodilatory than DHPs)

Most cardiac effect of all CCBs
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Constipation — most common ADR (classic!)
Bradycardia, AV block, hypotension
↑ Digoxin levels (P-glycoprotein inhibition)

CI: HFrEF (worsens systolic function)
CI: WPW + Afib (can accelerate accessory pathway)
CI: Combined with β-blocker (additive AV block)
AVOID: digoxin + verapamil (toxicity risk)
💊 Clinical Use
  • Variant angina (first-line) + chronic stable angina
  • SVT termination (IV — slows AV node)
  • Afib/flutter rate control (oral)
  • Hypertension + Afib (oral)
  • Migraine prophylaxis (alternative)
  • HCM (↓ LVOTO + improves diastolic filling)
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ Constipation = classic ADR — only CCB with this as primary complaint
⭐ Inhibits P-glycoprotein → ↑ digoxin plasma levels → check digoxin levels when starting verapamil
⭐ Only non-DHP + β-blocker = DANGEROUS → additive AV nodal suppression → complete heart block
⭐ Contraindicated in HFrEF (negative inotropy worsens systolic function)

⚠ TRAP Verapamil + β-blocker → complete heart block / asystole. NEVER combine. (Amlodipine + β-blocker is SAFE.)

⚠ TRAP Verapamil in WPW + Afib → blocks AV node → forces conduction through accessory pathway → VF risk
Diltiazem Non-DHP CCB · Intermediate effect PO or IV · QD–TID (formulation-dependent)
⚙ MOA
Non-DHP — blocks L-type Ca²⁺ channels in vascular smooth muscle AND cardiac tissue

Intermediate cardiac effect between verapamil (most cardiac) and DHPs (least cardiac)

↓ AV node conduction + ↓ SA node automaticity (less than verapamil)
Arterial vasodilation (less than DHPs)
↓ Contractility (less negative inotropic than verapamil)
⚠ Side Effects / CI
Bradycardia, AV block (less severe than verapamil)
Peripheral edema, headache
Fewer GI effects than verapamil

CI: HFrEF (↓ contractility)
CI: Combined with β-blocker
CI: WPW + Afib
Better tolerated than verapamil
💊 Clinical Use
  • Variant angina (first-line) + chronic stable angina
  • SVT rate control (IV)
  • Afib/flutter rate control (oral)
  • Hypertension
  • When verapamil causes intolerable constipation or side effects
  • HCM (similar to verapamil)
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ Intermediate cardiac effect: more cardiac than DHPs, less cardiac than verapamil
⭐ Clinically interchangeable with verapamil for most indications (Afib rate control, SVT, angina)
⭐ Fewer GI side effects than verapamil (no constipation as primary complaint)

⚠ TRAP Still contraindicated with β-blockers (same danger as verapamil — additive AV block) despite being "intermediate"

⚠ TRAP Both non-DHPs are CONTRAINDICATED in HFrEF — only amlodipine is safe. This is heavily tested.

Ranolazine — Late INa inhibitor (Unique MOA)

Ranolazine Late INa Inhibitor · 2nd-Line Add-On PO · ER · BID · 500–1000 mg
⚙ MOA
Inhibits late inward Na⁺ current (late INa) in ischemic myocytes

Ischemia → channels fail to inactivate → ↑ late INa → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ → Na/Ca exchanger (NCX) runs in reverse → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺ → diastolic dysfunction + ↑ wall tension → ↑ MVO₂

Ranolazine blocks this late INa → ↓ intracellular Na⁺/Ca²⁺ → ↓ diastolic tension → ↓ MVO₂

Does NOT affect HR or BP
⚠ Side Effects / CI
QTc prolongation — monitor ECG; avoid with other QT-prolonging drugs (amiodarone, sotalol, fluoroquinolones, azithromycin)
Dizziness, nausea, constipation, headache

Metabolism: CYP3A4 substrate
Drug interactions: azole antifungals, macrolide antibiotics → ↑ ranolazine levels

CI: Severe hepatic impairment
No dose adjustment in renal failure
💊 Clinical Use
  • Chronic stable angina — add-on when other drugs insufficient or not tolerated
  • Ideal for: angina + hypotension (no BP effect), angina + bradycardia (no HR effect)
  • Angina + COPD/asthma (cannot use non-selective β-blockers)
  • Microvascular angina — effective (where nitrates are less so)
  • NOT for acute angina attacks (not fast enough onset)
⭐ HY Facts + ⚠ Traps
⭐ Only antianginal that does NOT change HR or BP — hemodynamically neutral
⭐ MOA: inhibits late INa → ↓ intracellular Ca²⁺ overload → ↓ MVO₂ (mechanistically unique)
⭐ Perfect for hemodynamically limited patients (hypotension, bradycardia) who can't tolerate other antianginals
⭐ Effective in microvascular angina (where nitrates are less effective)
⭐ Second-line / add-on — not first-line monotherapy; always used alongside other antianginals

⚠ TRAP Ranolazine prolongs QTc — do NOT combine with other QT-prolonging agents

⚠ TRAP CYP3A4 substrate — azole antifungals + macrolide antibiotics significantly ↑ ranolazine plasma levels → toxicity
